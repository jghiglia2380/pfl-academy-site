<\!-- Section 2: Jobs vs. Careers -->

Jobs vs. Careers

A job is a position of employment that provides income, typically with specific duties and responsibilities. Jobs are often seen as short-term or temporary, and may not always align with your long-term goals or interests.

A career, on the other hand, is a long-term professional journey that typically involves progression, skill development, and a series of related jobs that build upon one another. Careers often align with personal interests, values, and goals.

Key Differences Between Jobs and Careers

Aspect Job Career
Time Frame Short-term, temporary Long-term, ongoing
Focus Income, immediate needs Growth, development, fulfillment
Education May require minimal training Often requires ongoing education/training
Personal Investment Lower emotional investment Higher personal commitment
Progression Limited advancement path Clear advancement opportunities

Case Study: Jordan's Journey

Jordan worked part-time at a coffee shop during college (a job). After graduation, they got an entry-level position at a marketing firm (beginning of career). Over the next ten years, Jordan advanced to senior marketing manager through a series of promotions and by changing companies twice to gain broader experience. Each position built upon skills from the previous role, creating a cohesive career path in marketing.

Jordan's coffee shop position was a job that provided income during school, but wasn't connected to their long-term goals. Their marketing positions formed a career with progression, skill development, and alignment with their interests.

<\!-- Section 3: Career Exploration -->

Career Exploration

Effective career exploration involves understanding yourself, researching options, and making informed decisions about your future path. Let's explore some key components of career exploration.

Self-Assessment

Before exploring career options, it's important to understand your own interests, skills, values, and personal preferences. Self-assessment helps you identify careers that might be a good match.

Interests

What subjects, activities, or topics do you enjoy?

  • What do you like to do in your free time?
  • What subjects in school do you find most engaging?
  • What topics do you enjoy reading or learning about?

Skills

What are you good at doing?

  • What tasks come naturally to you?
  • What do others compliment you on?
  • What abilities have you developed through practice?

Values

What's important to you in work and life?

  • What do you want your work to accomplish?
  • What kind of work environment do you prefer?
  • How important is work-life balance, income, recognition, etc.?

Personal Preferences

What conditions help you thrive?

  • Do you prefer working with people, data, or things?
  • Do you like structured or flexible environments?
  • Do you prefer leading or supporting others?

Career Research Resources

Once you have a better understanding of yourself, you can begin exploring careers that align with your interests, skills, and values. Here are some valuable resources:

Online Resources

  • O*NET OnLine (onetonline.org) - Comprehensive database of occupational information
  • Bureau of Labor Statistics (bls.gov) - Information on job outlook, earnings, and required education
  • CareerOneStop (careeronestop.org) - Career exploration tools and resources
  • MyNextMove (mynextmove.org) - User-friendly tool to explore careers

Activity: Self-Assessment Reflection

Take some time to reflect on your interests, skills, values, and preferences. Write down:

  1. Three activities or subjects you genuinely enjoy
  2. Three skills or abilities you believe you're good at
  3. Three values that are important to you in a future career
  4. Based on these reflections, list 2-3 career fields that might interest you
<\!-- Section 4: Skill Builder -->

Skill Builder: Career Path Planner

Career Path Planner

Now that you've learned about jobs vs. careers and explored your own interests and skills, it's time to create a comprehensive career development plan. This plan will help you map out your short-term, mid-term, and long-term career goals.

Instructions:

  1. Choose a target career field based on your interests and skills
  2. For each time frame (short-term, mid-term, long-term), set specific goals in all five categories
  3. Make sure your goals are SMART (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound)
  4. Consider how each goal builds toward your overall career development
Launch Career Path Planner

Your completed Career Path Planner will become part of your portfolio and can be updated as your goals and interests evolve.
